# Geography & Hiding

> Part of the [Rural Hide and Seek Ruleset](./README.md)

---

## Map Scale

Rural play requires a **significantly larger map** than urban play. A single county can be the size of a major city's metro area.

### Recommended Scales

| Game Length | Suggested Radius | Area Type |
|-------------|-----------------|-----------|
| 2–3 hours | 25-mile radius | Single county |
| 4–6 hours | 50-mile radius | 2–3 counties |
| Full day | 75–100-mile radius | Regional (multiple counties or a small state) |

The map boundary should be agreed upon before the game starts. Print or screenshot the map and share with all players.

---

## Boundaries

Replace transit zone boundaries with:

- **County lines** — The most natural rural boundary. Crossing into another county requires a Crossing Card.
- **State lines** — For large multi-state games; crossing requires a special State Line Card.
- **Natural features** — Rivers, lakes, mountain ridges, and state forests create natural hard barriers.
- **Highway numbers** — "Stay east of US-281" or "stay north of State Highway 16" are clean, verifiable boundaries.

### Prohibited Areas
The following are always out of bounds regardless of card plays:
- Private property without prior permission from the landowner
- Active agricultural operations (fields with machinery, feedlots, orchards during harvest)
- Tribal land or protected wilderness without the proper permits
- Interstate highway medians or restricted-access areas

## Hiding Spots

In rural environments, the landscape offers different hiding opportunities than cities.

### Types of Rural Hiding Spots

| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| **Infrastructure** | Water towers, grain elevators, grain silos, feed mills |
| **Small Towns** | Post offices, general stores, town squares, volunteer fire stations |
| **Roads** | Named gravel roads, numbered county roads, highway rest stops |
| **Natural** | State park trailheads, fishing access points, lake boat ramps |
| **Historical** | Roadside historical markers, old cemeteries, abandoned depots |
| **Commercial** | Feed & seed stores, farm co-ops, rural diners, roadside stands |

### The Smallest Town Mechanic

One of the best rural hiding strategies is the **Smallest Town** rule:

> **Rule:** A Hider may declare "Smallest Town" when they are within the incorporated limits of the least-populated town in the play area (verified by most recent Census data). While there, Seekers must spend one extra Range Card before they can play a Location Card.

This encourages exploration of obscure, hard-to-find communities and rewards preparation.

## Population Density Considerations

- **Advantage for Hiders:** Low density means fewer witnesses and more obscure hiding spots.
- **Disadvantage for Hiders:** An unfamiliar vehicle parked on a gravel road is memorable to locals.
- **Seeker tip:** Ask locals if they've seen an unfamiliar vehicle — rural communities notice strangers.

## GPS & Navigation

- All players should have GPS navigation available (phone or dedicated device).
- Pre-download offline maps for the play area in case of poor cell service.
- Satellite/aerial imagery layers (Google Maps, Apple Maps, or Gaia GPS) help both teams evaluate terrain.

---

## Setting Up the Play Area

Before the game:
1. Agree on the outer boundary and mark it on a shared map (Google My Maps works well).
2. Identify prohibited areas and mark them.
3. Confirm all players have offline maps downloaded.
4. Note fueling and charging stations within the play area — they become strategic resources.
5. Generate driving isochrones from the starting point (see [`tools/`](../tools/)).
